### Why does this work? How can it grow and be sustained?

Many of the assumptions on which Sun Hydraulics is built seem unusual and perhaps even a bit nutty. Further, as organizations grow and expand—perhaps even globalize—they often become more formal. So what is your reaction/analysis to the overall design, principles, practices, and structure at Sun Hydraulics? Will it be possible to sustain Sun without making fundamental changes in its design? What would you foresee to be the main facilitators and inhibitors of its ability to grow and be sustained?

Question 1:

The case of Sun Hydraulics is a fascinating example of one person sticking to their beliefs and bucking trends. Noticing how to properly motivate and incentivize employees is a major -if not the major- challenge in a functioning organization. Bob Koski, the founder and CEO of Sun Hydraulics, set out from the beginning to behave differently.

Mr. Koski was a pioneer in "Horizontal Management." As he set out to start his organization he decided that a hierarchy in a company is inherently counterproductive. While most large companies have several layers of abstraction from the CEO, Sun Hydraulics did not. This allowed for employee communication to go up, as there was no artificial pressure of impressing a hire-up. Cross-collaboration was highly encouraged, and formal meetings were abolished. Employees were more free to have spontaneous conversations, allowing for thoughts to more freely turn into products.

==The goals were simple enough: establish an organization where employees felt empowered to succeed, where they are motivated, possess the right abilities (or eagerness to learn), and in an environment where they are creative.[2]== One example of this is to eliminate per-part quotas and bonus’s from the production line. While this may sound like it would disincentivize employees, it actually empowered them to focus on their work and employ their own methods of production. In the long run I am sure that this approach helped to improve both quality of life of the employee (since they aren’t pushing hard and burning out to get the bonus), but also the quality of the product since they are now under less time pressure. ==Incorporating rigorous testing at the end of the process validated that theory.==

From an environmental point of view, by allowing the workers to set their own schedule, form their own committees and "natural clusters", and eliminating "intimidation roles"[1], the employees felt secure, a sense of belonging and freedom to self-actualize. These are all tenets of Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs, defining what makes a person feel whole.

For possession of the right abilities, employees at Sun Hydraulics were allowed to take part in new roles and experiences, a process we now call "Job Crafting" [3]which allowed them to have freedom and flexibility in their job description. This helped the employees feel empowered, and in-turn were more productive.

In my opinion, this is an incredible way of doing business. At the end of the day, it is a companies job to make sure that their product is high quality and sells well at a profit. Everything else is just what is required to support that goal. This leads to many companies forgetting to value their employees, since they are really just the means to accomplish a profit. Sun Hydraulics did an amazing job at valuing their employees, and ultimately succeeding as a company.
