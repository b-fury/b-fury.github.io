    1. Globalization
    
    Globalization has greatly affected my company. I work for an international company headquartered in Massachusetts. First and foremost, my company, Thermo Fisher Scientific (Thermo for short), is a multinational conglomerate that spans many different industries and applications. In any given week I am communicating with people in Germany and India, and all of us have more or less the same goals of new product development. One phrase that stuck out in the readings, was that "You don't globalize companies until you globalize people" [1]. By that metric Thermo is truly a global company. At any given time, we have people from all over the world working at facilities spread across the globe, not only in their home country, but also in other countries as beneficial. As a byproduct of purchasing and integrating successful companies in their field, Thermo has created an organization with subject matter experts all working together as needed. This means that radiation detection experts in Germany are talking to our sustaining engineering team in India, all while the portable analytical instruments group here in the States is incorporating each other groups ideas. We are an Interdependent organization, and there is just simply no way that a change in India or Germany would not directly affect my day-to-day. Successful integration of these teams is a great example of both mindful communication and multicultural teaming.

3. Personal Experience

As iI have discussed above, I have a fairly comprehensive background in multicultural interactions. My company is set up to be quite process oriented, and we are all constantly reminded to continuously improve. This has allowed each team member to individually feel empowered to work on communication, and provides a shared cognitive schema for us to work with. Each team has a varied communication approach, however. For most presentations from my German colleagues, we tend to get more data-driven decision making process, whereas those in India tend to describe things more vaguely. This is captured quite nicely in the GAP, with indirect and direct communication styles. In order to overcome that, we in the US are required to steer each team to report out in a more neutral fashion, combining both a data-driven approach and a more talk-based, narrative approach. In order to do this successfully we have to go into each meeting assuming that both sides of the coin will need to be addressed. This means that we go in with a group of perceptions and assumptions to get to where we need to. For instance, I know that I will have to really press our India team to commit to a piece of data or requirement, and I also know that I will need to press the German team to provide more commentary for their part.
