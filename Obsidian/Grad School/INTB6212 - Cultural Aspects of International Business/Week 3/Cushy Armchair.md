The course of action that I chose is to fly to each site and meet with each regional leader. The purpose of that path was to really get to know the individual cultures and to build a coalition.

When Alison was brought on board to integrate the new company, she was faced with a multi-domestic organization, with offices worldwide that were essentially fully autonomous. The goal moving forward was to make a globally standardized organization. Initially, the new globalization strategy was communicated via email. For many cultures this approach far too impersonal, and does not match the preferred communication style of each office worldwide. After deciding to fly to many sites and learn from the managers, it was decided that the globalization push will be postponed. What instead happened was to start a pilot program in France and Singapore that combined both the benefits of global integration, while also helping to maintain local responsiveness, changing the organizational structure to be more of a transnational organization.

This program was a great success. Since both of these cultures are more risk averse than we are here in the States [1], it was great to start there and show these facilities that this process can in fact help. In fact, Both of these countries are also more relationship-based. This meant that the face-to-face time that was spent was truly a great option, and was a very smart approach.

Tightly integrating these companies, however, was not free of barriers. Even after seeing the great response to the pilot program, some managers in other countries just simply decided to not follow the new processes and methods. Unfortunately, those employees were dismissed, and replaced with a more open-minded person who was an internal champion for globalization. Another method to break through these barriers was to change the compensation methods, which also proved successful. All in all this was a very successful path, and now these two companies are performing well and integrated nicely.
