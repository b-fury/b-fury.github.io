As a background, globalization has a number of meanings, but for the sake of this conversation I am taking it to mean “the process of becoming a company that is successfully operating in several different countries spanning the globe”. This is a bit of a self-assigned definition, but it was be refined throughout this paper. The benefits of globalization a numerous, allowing for increased economies of scale, more diverse personnel and experiences, and increasing global wages. Cultural Diversity and Global Management

So how do you start becoming a global company (from a management perspective, not growth)? The first step is to learn about each country you are operating in. Let’s first talk about culture. Culture is not one’s race or nationality, culture is the customs and styles of a certain community (Robinson). For instance, my fiancé is American by birth and nationality, but her culture is that of the Ashkenazi Jewish people. These are two entirely different groups of people, and yet she is both. Her cultural norms and communication methods are not that of a standard American but reflect the values she was brought up with in her culture.  This raises a very interesting question: how does a global manager know what to do? There are several options as to how to approach this. For instance, in order to successfully integrate a culture, you must first try to understand the culture and its norms.

It can be very challenging as both a new manager and an experienced one when faced with a new culture. In order to get a better handle on learning about your employees and what motivates them, there are several tools to help map cultural business practices. One of these tools is Global Manager. Global manager is a crowdsourced database of people in different countries that help to show what exactly makes them tick.  I have attached in Figure 1 at the rear of this paper an example of what the output from Global Manager looks like. As you can see this program distills employees into categories and rates them along a scale. Each culture (and in the case of Figure 1, a school group), is then grouped together and the sum of their answers helps to frame how each group is motivated.

While these tools are useful, I have learned in this course that it is very easy to oversimplify a person or culture based on these metrics. While one can adequately predict behavior of a large group with data from a large group, it is unfair at best, and prejudice at worst, to assume that the individual acts as the group. Andy Molinsky has a great article about this, and in that he states:

“But we’re often surprised to discover that the person in question acts in a completely different way from how we anticipated. Instead of being reticent, our colleague from Asia is actually quite loud and confrontational. Instead of behaving aggressively, our Israeli supplier is mild-mannered. And as we encounter various other people who confound our expectations about cultural differences, we wonder where we went wrong.”

This so greatly encapsulates the issues at hand. We can have all the tools in the world available to us, and we can form all of the researched opinions about a culture, but at the end of the day, we are all individuals. Interestingly enough sometimes even having all of this information available to you can’t help you from making the wrong decisions. Such was the case with Arla foods.

As we saw in the Arlo foods case, it is simply not possible to apply the values and motivators of the headquarters country, you must be willing and able to accept and understand the perspectives of those in your new territories.

The Arlo case was really impactful for me. Throughout the case, it became apparent that each player was acting in what they thought was the best interest of the company. Of course, they were acting with their own biases based on their cultural upbringing. The mistakes made were exactly the sort I would have made before taking this class. If I were running an American company, I would have without doubt supported the freedom of speech and ended up in a similar place as the Arlo executives. The nuances associated with how to truly understand the culture would’ve been lost on me. Now I know that part of choosing to do business in a country means that you not only have to accept the countries culture, but you have to embrace the good and bad, and be an advocate for all those you employ.
