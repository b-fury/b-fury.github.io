I am certainly a believer in Free Speech; however, I think that it has its consequences. There is a very, very fine line between hate speech and what was published. I do not condone the killing of anyone, and certainly publishing offensive content should not be grounds for murder. Limiting speech certainly goes against the entire concept of free speech, but that doesn’t mean that every word is for every venue. There is no law stating that freedom of speech guarantees freedom from consequences from the general public. Just because something can be said, published, or produced without criminal action does not mean that there wouldn’t be an outcry. In the case of Arlo Foods this meant that they are collateral damage.

Regarding David Brooks article, I think that he makes some great points about how we tend to handle some things here in America. I think that in situations like “The University of Illinois fir[ing] a professor who taught the Roman Catholic view on homosexuality,”[1] is a super interesting grey area. If the school is a state school, which presumably receives some form of state or federal funding, then I think that they should not be able to pick and choose who gets a podium. I am not suggesting that they should be allowed to incite violence, or other attacks on the public, but I definitely think that all viewpoints should be allowed to speak. In the case of Ayaan Hirsi Ali no one is forcing anyone to go to see these speeches [2], but the university is a private institution, so they can enforce whatever policy they want. I also think that provocateurs are entirely eligible to be deplatformed [3] and can be condemned for their actions by private institutions, again, they should be allowed to have a public forum. I don’t think I am Charlie.

Arla Foods is collateral damage, but they could’ve handled this way better. A senior executive at Arla said: “But I don't think things will get that bad.” [4] This was incredibly shortsighted. To think that a growing boycott movement would not affect them is nothing short of hubris, and cultural misunderstanding. Arla simply did not appropriately map the situation. Arla executives should have reached out to members of the affected communities and see how they can help. They should have empowered participation and engaged with Islamic leaders and Saudi citizens. Once they hear back from the community, they should work to find a common ground, maybe agreeing that the image was insensitive, and help to build back trust. Finally, I think that Arla took a very passive role, citing that they are helpless “What can we do? We can’t edit newspapers, we can’t comment on government actions, we can’t get involved in politics and we certainly can’t address religion.” Which shows that they do not truly understand the issue at hand, which is that they did not take into consideration the passion and efficacy of the movement and didn’t adjust course to try to actually be a part of the Islamic community they think they’re a part of. You can be a large company and navigate these waters well. It all simply starts with outreach and understanding.

1. **Arla Foods Case B (Initial post by Day 6; Reply by Day 7)**
    
    1. What is your reaction to what happened in the (B) case?
    2. What, if any, are the implications of the Arla situation for your business or career?
    
    It would not have been my first guess that a women's group would have formed an additional set of protests against Arla. It highlights how much we are globally interconnected, but still so culturally diverse. The waters are so tough to navigate, but at some point it was a conscious choice for Arla to go after the Saudi Arabian market. While I am sure that this crisis was not anticipated in any way, it is entirely fair for those who do not support Saudi treatment of women to be upset with Arla. Again, this is another example of shortsightedness on the part of Arla executives. A serious argument could be made that Arla should have never done business in Saudi Arabia, since they do not reflect the same values as those in Denmark. As far as the rest of the response, I think that it was ultimately handled quite well:
    
    “First: Arla Foods Company strongly denounces and condemns the cartoons published by the Danish newspapers deriding the prophet, peace and blessings be upon him. It also rejects the newspaper’s justification for that incident."
    
    This is exactly what they needed to say, very effective and direct.
    
    As far as implications for my career, I don't think that I will find myself in a similar situation. I can say, though, that this will make me more conscious of my actions and more aware of potential consequences.
