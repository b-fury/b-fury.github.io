### Question 2

Income inequality has been, and will continue to be, a critical economic issue and discussion throughout our lifetimes. There is an overall issue of those at the top having incomprehensible wealth, while those in the middle class and lower class are getting squeezed out of wealth by offshoring. There will continue to be a class system; overtime we will see a further lessening of wealth in the bottom 90% and further striations in wealth belonging there. For instance, current patterns seem to suggest that upper income wealth is increasing dramatically, roughly doubling over my lifetime. [1]

This raises greater questions with the status of those in the middle class. As wealth becomes harder to attain, we are simultaneously seeing other items, such as healthcare becoming just as scarce. There are many, many workers in our economy today who work as gig-workers (or private contractors). These people have far more limited access to healthcare, and are seeing high healthcare premiums since they have no employer to rely on as a collective bargaining unit. Data is also suggesting that “Middle-class whites are experiencing a higher mortality rate from issues such as alcoholism, suicide and drug overdose.” [1] This further suggests that people are having a harder time getting treatment for their ailments or are increasingly finding ways to escape their current standing.

I believe that these issues can be solved with guaranteed income, with one caveat. Fundamentally I feel that free access to healthcare is critically important to us as a society as time goes on. Guaranteed Income experiments have been run in many countries having mixed success [2], with some countries, such as Finland, finding that it did not improve life in a statistically significant way, whereas in countries such as Namibia it has been successful [3]. I think the factors here are two-fold. Namibia is a less wealthy country overall, but also it has less access to healthcare. Increasing basic income there may very well allow people greater access to healthcare and other essentials.  This leads to almost suggesting a universal wealth program would be best globally. Providing access (via government or NGO) to ensure all people have access to essential services at no direct costs will be the most effective program for improving quality of life. In Finland’s case, there is already a large amount of social services provided to residents, whereas in Namibia, there are almost none. I feel that is the gap that needs to be filled, and if a guaranteed income scheme can provide that, than I believe it is a necessity as time goes on.
