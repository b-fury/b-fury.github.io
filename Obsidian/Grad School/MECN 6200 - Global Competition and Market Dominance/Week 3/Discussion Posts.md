  

### Discussion 1

1. What does Ms. McCloskey believe is responsible for the vast wealth in the U.S? Do you agree?
2. Do you believe your children will be better off than you are?

  

In Ms. McCloskey’s words: “The Great Enrichment of the past two centuries has one primary source: the liberation of ordinary people to pursue their dreams of economic betterment” [1].

What I believe this means in practice is that each person’s desire to enrich themselves has led to many valuable creations. This, in turn, allowed for profits and economic benefits to skyrocket in a self-fulfilling cycle. By viewing each human as a consumer, and therefore equal, the world has greatly improved. I think this is a very interesting viewpoint, definitely looking back at history with a certain tint, and even with our current world under the same lens. There are still those who are essentially economic slaves [2], and the author’s declaration in the opening paragraphs that quality of life is greatly improved is one of privilege. Yes, real wages have increased, but that does not mean that the actual quality of life to gain those wages has, and it is pure fallacy to assume that those who don’t like those working conditions can simply just get a better job. While China is an extreme, this also plays out in the US. That aside, the notion that all consumers are created equal, and all people are entitled to seek their own betterment allows for the economy to keep growing and personal (as well as national) wealth to expand.

I, personally, have greatly benefitted from economic liberation. I grew up in a poor household and paid my own way through college. I am now a successful engineer, responsible for directly creating and inventing those products Ms. McCloskey references throughout.  I add new products to the market and expand the markets that some products serve. In this lens, I have not only increased spending, but I have personally injected money into the economy by increasing exports to the US, and increasing my own wages and spending potential.

### Discussion 2

1. How has Apple continued to be able to sell its phones at higher prices and quantities?

Apple has long made a name for itself as being a leader in design and forward thought. Starting with the Macintosh, Apple relied heavily on pushing themselves as a counterculture brand [2], initially rebelling against IBM. After a dramatically awful decade in the 1990s, Apple produced a hit product in the iPod. This led to a large amount of brand satisfaction and halo customers [3]. These decisions ultimately led to a very successful product, and probably the most successful smartphone brand, the iPhone.

At first the market was established with carrier subsidies, allowing for consumers to only see that the smartphone cost $199 [4], but eventually the subsidy model went away [5] and consumers were left to see the real cost of their phone. What apple was able to do in the next 5 years was a “brilliant stroke of Product Management “[1]. Apple successfully segmented their market into several groups: the “low-price” tier who want an iPhone but it can be a few years old, a “mid-range” tier that want the _newest_ phone, but it doesn’t have to be the best, and a “luxury/high-end” tier which wants the latest and greatest phone. Each tier in itself has different pricing options, and ultimately, Apple was able to take a product that started at $649, and expand it all the way into a product that can cost $1399 [6] without cannibalizing sales. What Apple has done is define different demand curves at each tier, and successfully provided products that fit most, if not all, of those equilibrated price points.  As the global smartphone market saturates, differentiation will not only be in price, but quality of product. Apple has also differentiated their product by using higher end materials, and putting more effort into easy to use software, allowing for its most high-end users to feel satisfied with their luxury product just as much as those who need their inexpensive workhorse.
