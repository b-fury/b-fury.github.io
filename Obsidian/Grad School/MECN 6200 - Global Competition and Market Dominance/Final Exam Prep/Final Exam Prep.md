### Returns to Scale

Returns to scale describe how quantity changes when all inputs change **proportionately**.

1. If %Δ in output equals the %Δ in inputs then:_Constant Returns to Scale_
2. If %Δ in output exceeds the %Δ in inputs then:_Increasing Returns to Scale_
3. If %Δ in output is less than the %Δ in inputs then:_Decreasing Returns to Scale_

### Market Concentration Ratio

$Market\ Concentration\ Ratio\ = {\Sigma_{ Sales\ Industry\ top\ 4\ firms} \over Industry\ Sales}$

### Herfindahl index

$H=∑ s^2 * 10,000$

- Post-merger H index below 1000:
    - Unconcentrated Market, no scrutiny from regulators
- Post-merger H index between 1000 and 1800:
    - Moderately Concentrated Market
        - <100 increase in HHI: no scrutiny
        - >100 increase in HHI: potentially raise significant competitive concerns
- Post-merger H index above 1800:
    - Highly Concentrated Market
        - <50: No scrutiny
        - 50<x<100: potentially raise significant competitive concerns
        - >100: likely to create or enhance market power or facilitate its exercise

### Unfair Competition

For the first time, in August 2015, the Federal Trade Commission defined what unfair competition is. Its power derives from Section 5 of the FTC Act which says that unfair methods of competition are illegal.

- The injury must be substantial. The commission is not concerned with trivial or merely speculative harms.
- The injury must not be outweighed by any offsetting consumer or competitive benefits that the sales practice also produces.
- The injury must be one that consumers could not reasonably have avoided.

### Short-Run Costs

In the short run, firms operate as long as price exceeds their average variable cost (AVC):

- If P > ATC the firm is profitable, π 0.
- If AVC < P < ATC then π < 0, but π is greater than if Q = 0 (The firm would still shut down.)
- If P < AVC then π < 0, and π is less than if the firm were to shut down. ← **Shutdown Rule**

### Diseconomies of scale

  

The process of unionizing workers is expensive. Unionizing large firms is less expensive than doing the same for small firms. So, large firms may face diseconomies due to unions. Large firms tend to install layers of management creating higher costs than several small firms would face. Another source of diseconomies is due to resource limitations. Consider the great restaurant you go to: Could the head chef handle two restaurants?

Economies of scope exist if savings are achieved by producing a wider range of goods, for example; NEU has a BA in Business and an MBA program. When there are economies of scope, firms tend to produce a variety of products. Positive scope economies are present if an existing firm can add a new product at a lower cost than the same product being added by a new firm.

**Rental Cars and Gasoline**

Consider how to price a gallon of gasoline on rental cars returned with less than a full tank of gas.

Option 1: Return the car with a full tank of gasoline. Local gasoline is $3 dollars per gallon.Option 2: Prepay at $2.25 per gallon (on which gallons?)Option 3: Buy gas when the car is returned at $7 dollars a gallon.

- Question: Which option produces the biggest profit and how does the consumer view these choices?
- Answer: Profits depend on what the consumer decides, but if most consumers return a car with no gas in it then Option 3 is the most profitable for the company. Consumers are uncertain how many miles they will drive and what mileage the car gets. Many consumers choose option 2 and then return the car with nearly a full tank.
