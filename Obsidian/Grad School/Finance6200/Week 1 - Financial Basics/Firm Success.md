A company's value depends on three major factors:

1. How much cash is earned by the firm—more cash is preferred to less. Therefore, the higher the cash earned by a company, the greater its value.
2. When the cash is earned—earlier is preferred to later. Thus, the earlier the cash is earned, the greater the firm's value.
3. The uncertainty surrounding the expected cash flow—the more certain we are about the cash earned by the firm (that is, the lower the uncertainty or risk of the cash flow), the greater the firm's value.

### Why Should our Objective Remain to Maximize the Firm's Value?

Shareholders are residual claimants.First, shareholders are residual claimants. To understand this, imagine a large pot full of the money generated by the company. Then, imagine a line of everyone with a right to receive some of the cash. These people are known as claimants because they have a claim on the cash generated by the company. They include suppliers (payment for goods and services procured), employees (wages and benefits), lenders (interest and loan repayments), the community at large (community development and charitable activities), government (taxes), and shareholders (dividends). The last person in this line is the shareholder. This means that shareholders do not receive anything until all the other claimants have been paid in full. Thus, we refer to shareholders as residual claimants, that is, they receive the residue, or what is left after everyone else is paid. Our goal is to maximize the size of the pot (that is, the firm's overall value). However, since the other claimants are paid before shareholders, and their claims are typically fixed by contract between themselves and the firm, we can state this goal differently as maximizing the residual that goes to shareholders. As you can see, the only way we can maximize the residual is to maximize the pot size. Thus, the two goals become equivalent. At this point, I should add that while maximizing shareholder wealth is equivalent to maximizing overall firm value in theory, it is not necessarily true in practice. For it to be true in practice, we need at least two main conditions. First, managers reveal all pertinent information about the firm to investors (that is, the stock market) and second, investors correctly use the information quickly to increase or reduce the firm's stock price, as the case may be. In general, these conditions are reasonably satisfied (for most developed economies); so, we can say that the two objectives are equivalent in practice.

### The Agency Relationship

The second justification is perhaps more fundamental. Look up who owns any major company in the U.S., and you will find that the managers own relatively little to nothing of the company's stock. For example, as of February 2009, the executive officers and directors of General Electric owned 5,260,945 of the 10,569,000,000 shares outstanding (less than 0.05%). What this means is that these executives control a company that they do not own. When someone makes decisions on behalf of someone else, we have what is called an agency relationship. The person making decisions is called the agent, and the person on whose behalf the decisions are being made is called the principal. For most public companies, managers run the company on behalf of shareholders. Thus, we will say that managers are shareholders' agents. In agency relationships, agents are required to act in their principals' best interest. Hence, managers must act in the shareholders' best interest. While shareholders might have many diverse (and potentially conflicting) interests concerning their investment in the company, it is safe to presume that all shareholders are interested in seeing the stock price go up. Thus, if managers are to act in the shareholders' best interest, they should strive to maximize the stock's value.

### The Agency Problem

I said earlier that an agent is required to act in the principal's best interest. You are probably thinking that agents do not always do this, and you are right! Since agents are people with their own self-interests, sometimes an agent does something that benefits himself or herself to the principal's detriment. Economists refer to this possibility as the agency problem. In the context of the modern corporation, this means that managers sometimes make decisions or take actions that benefit themselves while hurting shareholders. Can you think of some examples, perhaps at your place of employment or as reported by the press?

Agency problems are pervasive, and principals act to prevent or minimize them. The costs of such preventive actions are called agency costs. In general, these preventive actions fall into two categories: incentives and monitoring. You can think of these as the carrot and the stick. Incentives are the carrots, and monitoring is the stick.

### **Incentives**

[![](https://lmscontent.embanet.com/NEU/FINA6200/Files/w1_media05.jpg)](https://lmscontent.embanet.com/NEU/FINA6200/Files/w1_media05.jpg)

Incentives are programs designed to align the agent's interests with those of the principal. They are built on the premise that agents will try to maximize their own benefits. Therefore, if we arrange things such that the agent maximizes the principal's benefits whenever he tries to maximize his own benefits, then everyone wins. For example, we know that managers would prefer more compensation to less. Therefore, we can tie their compensation to the company's performance so that the company must perform better in order for managers to receive higher compensation. This is the underlying idea behind stock options and restricted stock grants, bonuses, and other forms of incentive compensation.

### **Monitoring**

This refers to efforts expended to oversee the agent to ensure that he is acting in the principal's interest. Therefore, shareholders can keep an eye on managers through the board of directors, auditors, and sometimes direct intervention. The goal here is to maintain a watchful eye over managers so that the opportunity they have to harm shareholders' interest is greatly diminished. Other forms of monitoring rely on the market, specifically the market for corporate control, also known as the takeover market. If managers do a poor job with the company, this will be reflected in the company's stock price. The lower the stock price, the greater the likelihood that another company will find it attractive to acquire the company and realize value by firing the incumbent managers. Therefore, we say that the takeover market provides additional monitoring for managerial behavior.

There is no guarantee that either incentives or monitoring will work, and it often seems that managers are relentlessly seeking ways of defeating shareholder attempts to rein them in or otherwise align the interests of the two parties.
