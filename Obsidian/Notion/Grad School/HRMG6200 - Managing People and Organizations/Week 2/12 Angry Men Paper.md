The brilliance of 12 angry men is that we, the audience, were trapped in a room for roughly 90 minutes with the men of a jury. The movie moved at a pace that made it feel like real time. We are taken on a voyage as 12 jurors discuss the fate of a suspect we never see. A young man is accused of murdering his father. Initially, to the audience, it seems an open and shut case where the suspect is clearly guilty, with irrefutable evidence. However, despite 11 out of the 12 jurors initially advocating for the suspect’s guilt, the suspect will ultimately be found not guilty.

This is a master class in not only incredible influence, but also an incredible study of bias, both inherent and cognitive. As rational points are made, these jurors refuse to acknowledge what is presented to them in a clear, concise manner. A significant number of the jurors had preconceived notions of what a kid growing up in the slums was like and that blinded them to the facts shown to them. Over time we see the other jurors agreeing with the questions that Juror 8 has, and ultimately, all are left with reasonable doubt that the suspect committed the murder.

The 4 most prominent influence techniques used in the movie are: coalition building, consultation, reason, and inspiration. Juror 8 calls for an anonymous second vote, this time stating that he would agree to change his verdict to guilty if no one voted not guilty. This was clearly a clever method of coalition building, allowing the people in the room to anonymously voice their alignment with the opinion of Juror 8. It was very effective, showing that at least one more vote was cast for not guilty, allowing for the debate to continue.

One juror mentioned that prosecution stated that there are no similar knives to the one purchased and allegedly used by the murderer. Juror 8 produces, rather surprisingly, a knife that looks very familiar to the accused's weapon, a rational step that led many jurors to have that specific argument be thrown out in their minds. By showing this knife, Juror 8 appeals to the sense of reason in the other jurors. This also very cleverly inserts further doubt in the prosecutors, opening the door to a sense that all facts that were presented could be disproven.

In the initial vote, only Juror 8 voted not guilty. His appeal was a simple one, that he was simply not convinced either way on a verdict. He used inspiration to appeal to each of their values, first appealing to their empathy "we wouldn't want to send a man to death immediately", a definitive example of inspiration.

Throughout the entirety of the movie, Juror 8 consults with the other jurors. There is one scene where Juror 8 asks Juror 4 a series of questions pertaining to things he has done recently. Juror 4 finds it egregious that the boy wouldn't know what movie he saw, but through this series of exchanges, it is revealed that Juror 4 does not, in fact, remember a large series of events in his own life, causing him to come around on the possibility the boy could have forgotten.

If you look at many of the methods of influence we see in our modules, in my opinion, there is one standout factor: reason. The appeal to reason is one of the strongest methods, since you're essentially leading others on a path you have written. Juror 8 is an intensely rational person. He is able to see through the testimony and see which points actually seem to hold merit. For instance, how could the man below even hear shouting when the train was going by? By appealing to each person familiar with the El train and allowing them to consider with their own experiences, Juror 8 proved logically that the El would simply be too loud if it were passing by exactly at the time of the stabbing as the female witness had stated. Logically, that holds merit, does Juror 8 know _factually_ that the train was too loud, no, but he knew just enough to insert _reasonable doubt_ in the mind of some other jurors.

I entirely disagree “All of this fighting is getting us nowhere.” The jurors were so incredibly focused on their own issues and biases that they were blind to the case at hand. By being calm, methodical, and rational Juror 8 was able to singlehandedly change this verdict. It took a long time, but eventually, the toughest juror to convince (Juror 3) realizes that he was not actually looking at the facts, but merely going with intuition and his biases based on his strained relationship with his son to determine his verdict. One other example of how fighting actually got them somewhere is the case of Juror 10. Juror 10 was making an angry plea when it is revealed that he has racial prejudice against the accused. This causes the entire room to turn their backs on Juror 10 and cause him to lash out now that no one is listening to him. This causes him to be isolated and angry, and ultimately causes him to rethink his position.

Overall, I feel like all 8 of the methods of influence are used in this film, and ultimately it is the influential nature of Juror 8 that spares the life of the accused. While I personally don’t believe that most of the methods used to change the verdict would be allowed in a courtroom, it is a very entertaining and insightful film showing that influential people can control how the world appears to others.
