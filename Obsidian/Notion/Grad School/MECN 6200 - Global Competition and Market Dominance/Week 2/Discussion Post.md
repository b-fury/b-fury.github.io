### Discussion 1

1. Is giving a raise to 27 million workers acceptable knowing that another 1.4 million workers will lose their jobs?
2. If companies are bankrupted by the higher wage cost, should these closed-down firms be compensated somehow?

  

The full impact of a raised minimum wage is extremely challenging to predict, at best, and a total shot in the dark at worst. Fortunately, sound economic principals developed over time can help to model these changes.

As estimated by the congressional budget office [1], 1.4 million people would lose their jobs, comparing to 900,000 people being brought above the poverty line. These are the easy predictions. The knock-on effects of raising wages are far-reaching and deep. This proposed wage change will affect areas such as Unemployment Compensation, and even Medicaid. What is most fascinating to me is that despite an increase in wages, there is a real effect of prices going up, passing the higher cost of goods right to the consumer, in effect reducing real income, despite nominal income increasing. However, this is not a linear relationship, as those with lower incomes still have a floor of expenses, and higher cash on hand will increase their spending. “Lower-income families spend a larger proportion of any additional income on goods and services than do families with higher income. That increased demand for goods and services would reduce the drop in employment for several years after the implementation of a higher minimum wage, CBO projects.” So while, some may lose their jobs, there will be a net decrease in unemployment over time. The demand curves for employees will be shifting at differing rates in different sectors. This could allow for (hypothetically) an increase in service workers, yet a decrease in lower paid manual laborers, both fields requiring the same cost of entry.

Some companies [2][3], have gone so far as to implement the higher wages now, before laws are enacted, as a gesture of goodwill, and increasing demand for workers to gain a competitive advantage.  By increasing demand when most workers are needed, they are effectively allowing themselves to run real-word models of how exactly an increase in minimum wage will playout before it becomes mandatory.

In the short term, many companies will see their expenses rise dramatically. In some cases, they may end up going bankrupt. If that is the case, in my opinion, these companies should not be compensated. The planned roll out of wage increases is over several years. This should give successful companies ample time to address costs, and change pricing in order to appropriately pay employees.

### Discussion 2:

https://blogs.wsj.com/economics/2016/02/12/low-inflation-which-complicates-fed-plans-cheers-consumers/

1. Why is the Fed worried about the lack of inflation?
2. Who benefits from low and falling prices?

The fed is worried about the low inflation rate because it may signal an economic downturn. Typically with low inflation comes an increase in unemployment, and slower job growth [1]. Also, with lower interest rates, the amount of overall money in the economy may shrink. If that happens, then the fed may be tempted to lower interest rates to add more money to the system. An issue with this is that interest rates are already historically low, and it is unknown how going even lower may affect the economy. Since the interest rate is now essentially at 0 [2], a negative interest rate will essentially disincentivize the use of conventional banks and may cause a run on cash and lowering savings.

A risk with higher rates of unemployment, specifically in 2021, is that the unemployment rate is already so high due to COVID-19 that the economy will slow down further and potentially cause a depression. Another very interesting factor is that while inflation is low, the stock market has been largely successful, showing a very unique divide between common economic indicators . With a lower interest rate we will see a decrease in real income over time as the cost of goods rise to meet the increased demand. This will affect companies who sell demanded goods, as they will need to increase stock to preserve pricing. Overall debt will go up, as money is cheaper to borrow, so ultimately creditors will benefit.
