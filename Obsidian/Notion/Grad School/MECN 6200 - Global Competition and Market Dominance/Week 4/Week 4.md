### Herfindahl Index (H)

Let Si be the market share of firm _i_

$H=∑Si^2$

The H index equals the sum of the squared market shares of all the firms in the industry. In this case, H ranges between 0 and 1. Some analysts multiply H by 10,000, in which case H ranges between 0 and 10,000

### Measuring Monopoly with the Herfindahl Index

$H=∑ s2 * 10,000$

Don't forget that, with other measures, the question of **which other firms are in the market** arises.

1. **Post-merger H index below 1000**. The Justice Department regards markets in this region to be unconcentrated. Mergers resulting in unconcentrated markets are unlikely to have adverse competitive effects and ordinarily require no further analysis.
2. **Post-merger H index between 1000 and 1800**. The Agency regards markets in this region to be moderately concentrated. Mergers producing an increase in the HHI of less than 100 points in moderately concentrated markets post-merger are unlikely to have adverse competitive consequences and ordinarily require no further analysis. Mergers producing an increase in the HHI of more than 100 points in moderately concentrated markets post-merger potentially raise significant competitive concerns.
3. **Post-merger H index above 1800**. The Agency regards markets in this region to be highly concentrated. Mergers producing an increase in the HHI of less than 50 points, even in highly concentrated markets post-merger, are unlikely to have adverse competitive consequences and ordinarily require no further analysis. Mergers producing an increase in the HHI of more than 50 points in highly concentrated markets post-merger potentially raise significant competitive concerns. Where the post-merger HHI exceeds 1800, it will be presumed that mergers producing an increase in the HHI of more than 100 points are likely to create or enhance market power or facilitate its exercise.

### Unfair Competition

- The injury must be substantial. The commission is not concerned with trivial or merely speculative harms.
- The injury must not be outweighed by any offsetting consumer or competitive benefits that the sales practice also produces.
- The injury must be one that consumers could not reasonably have avoided.

### Market Structure

  

$\bold {Lerner\,\,Index} = {(Price-MC)\over Price}$

### Perfect Competition

The principle characteristics of perfectly competitive industries are that they have many sellers who supply a homogeneous product to consumers without any loyalty to the seller. With so many sellers it is impossible for these industries to collude and coordinate their prices. The industry has free entry and exit with new firms attracted by excess short run profits. The Lerner Index is very low, in theory equaling zero.

### Price Determination: Competitive Industry

The price determined for the industry as a whole, P1, is the only price at which individual sellers in the industry can sell their product; it is also the only price that buyers are willing to pay.

Unless the industry price, which the competitive firm must accept, is greater than average cost, the firm will not make money.

### The Shutdown Rule:

In the short run, firms operate as long as price exceeds their average variable cost (AVC):

- If P > ATC the firm is profitable, π 0.
- If AVC < P < ATC then π < 0, but π is greater than if Q = 0 (The firm would shut shut down.)
- If P < AVC then π < 0, and π is less than if the firm were to shut down.

This third statement is referred to as the shutdown rule. Firms should not operate, in the short run, if they cannot cover their variable costs. So, they should shut down in the short run if P < AVC; in the long run they should shut down if π < 0.

### Profits: Competitive Industry

  

Looking at the cost curves' of the individual firm in the long run, the short and long run ATC will be tangent above the firm's output. MC passes through that point. At that output level, and at a price equal to P in the diagram, the firm earns no profit because P = ATC.

Note that in the long run, the firm operates at the minimum point of the both the short and long run ATC curves. That is, it cannot lower its costs either by operating more efficiently in the short run or by opening up a lower-cost plant in the long run.
