## Discussion 1

[http://www.wsj.com/articles/for-many-retailers-the-price-isnt-right-1482863716](http://www.wsj.com/articles/for-many-retailers-the-price-isnt-right-1482863716)

### Questions

1. How important are prices in the battle of brick and mortar stores against internet competitors? Are there other considerations that may be more important?
2. If these companies are striving to maximize their profits, does it make sense to ignore or reduce in its importance the pricing concept of MR=MC?

In the case of GNC[1], prices are seemingly the most important factor in winning the battle against the internet. Adding online shopping has taken them from having an oligopolistic position [2](GNC is one of a few companies in the vitamin/supplement brick-and-mortar space), to now having to compete with online retailers in what amounts to becoming pure competition. The only option once that happens is to drastically lower prices, because you are now uncompetitive. Vitamins are vitamins, they are easily commoditized, and it is very challenging to make a consumer believe that one brand is better than the other, which makes it quite easy to be undercut. For Restoration Hardware the problem was more challenging. They are a luxury/high end brand. For them to be successful, they need to display themselves as better than, or higher quality than other vendors. Interestingly enough, they have decided to lower prices up to 25% across the board, but only if you join their loyalty program. This is a very clever way for internet savvy consumers to get a great discount, while allowing those who prefer to just walk in and buy in person without research to still pay the higher prices to keep the brand image higher-end.

In the short term, they can attempt to stop paying attention to MR=MC. The assumption is that lower prices will increase sales volume, which will help to attract new customers. The hope is that in the long term those customers will stay despite pricing increasing. There is no way to consistently ignore MR=MC and be profitable.

## Discussion 2

[https://www.wsj.com/articles/peter-thiel-competition-is-for-losers-1410535536](https://www.wsj.com/articles/peter-thiel-competition-is-for-losers-1410535536)

### Questions

1. Competition is central to economic thought but what does it lead to in terms of profits?
2. Can investors avoid competitive firms?

Competition in its purest form causes no profits to be made at all [1]. This is due a number of factors, but centrally it is tied to MR=MC, if your business is running perfectly, you will keep making as many products as you will not lose money on. The side effect of this is that means that the profit made on your last remaining unit is 0, and when in pure competition the market has determined a _lower_ price than you would have if you were monopolistic.

I think it is almost impossible for investors to avoid investing in competitive firms. If you look at some companies, such as Apple, they have many business units, some of which are in a league of their own when it comes to holding monopolies. No one else makes the iPhone, they have a monopolistic position on the iOS app store, iOS software, and iPhone hardware. Admittedly, you could argue that they compete with the global smartphone market in general, but the software exclusivity puts it in a market of its own (much like the MacBook, and running macOS). However, they remain a firm that has large competition in the market in a number of other places. For instance, Apple just discontinued its Homepod smart speaker[2] because it simply did not compete with the offerings from other smart speaker manufacturers such as Amazon, and Google. This, I feel is the key to making good investments going forward, invest in competitive firms with monopolistic products (monopolistic competition) if you can't get in with one with a global monopoly exclusively.
