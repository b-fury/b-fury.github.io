**Types of Ratios**

For the following presentation, you will need to download the following document:  
Apple Inc.’s 2019 financial statements

We will

focus our discussion on five categories of financial ratios. These are:

1. Liquidity ratios
2. Asset management ratios
3. Debt management ratios
4. Profitability ratios
5. Market value ratios

We will illustrate these ratios using Apple’s 2019 financial statements as reported in its 2019 10-K filed  
with the SEC on 10/31/2019. These statements are contained in the accompanying Excel file. Ratios are  
also summarized in the Excel document, while the calculation and interpretation of each is discussed at  
the relevant point.

**Current Ratio**

The current ratio is calculated as the ratio of current assets to current liabilities:

𝐶𝑢𝑟𝑟𝑒𝑛𝑡 𝑅𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜 = 𝐶𝑢𝑟𝑟𝑒𝑛𝑡 𝐴𝑠𝑠𝑒𝑡𝑠  
𝐶𝑢𝑟𝑟𝑒𝑛𝑡 𝐿𝑖𝑎𝑏𝑖𝑙𝑖𝑡𝑖𝑒𝑠

In 2019, Apple’s current assets totaled $162.819 billion while its current liabilities totaled $105.718  
billion. Thus, its current ratio equals 162.819/105.718 = 1.54. This means that in 2019, Apple had $1.54  
available in cash and near cash assets for every dollar of currently due obligations. Is this good or bad?  
The correct answer is we don’t know. Generally speaking, we’d like to see the current ratio at least  
above 1.0 but this does not necessarily mean everything is OK if that’s the case or that things are bad if  
it’s not. We need to dig deeper to be able to reach an informed decision. One way to do this is to  
compare the same ratio for the same company over time. For Apple, its current ratio equaled 1.35 in  
2016, 1.28 in 2017, and 1.13 in 2018. Thus, Apple’s current ratio has declined over the past few years  
and the ratio for 2019 represents a reversal of that trend. We also can compare the calculated ratio to  
the industry average. In 2019, average current ratio for the computer and electronics industry was 2.57;  
thus, Apple appears to be below average on this ratio. Overall, it seems that the company’s short-term  
liquidity has declined somewhat over the previous three years and it’s below the industry average. We  
now can examine its financial statements further to better understand the forces behind the apparent  
decline in short-term liquidity.

**Quick Ratio**

The quick ratio is based on the idea that some current assets (notably inventories) may be difficult to  
convert into cash at their fair value if the company needs to raise money to pay its bills. Thus, the quick  
ratio ignores inventories in comparing due bills and resources available to pay them:

𝑄𝑢𝑖𝑐𝑘 𝑅𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜 = 𝐶𝑢𝑟𝑟𝑒𝑛𝑡 𝐴𝑠𝑠𝑒𝑡𝑠 − 𝐼𝑛𝑣𝑒𝑛𝑡𝑜𝑟𝑖𝑒𝑠  
𝐶𝑢𝑟𝑟𝑒𝑛𝑡 𝐿𝑖𝑎𝑏𝑖𝑙𝑖𝑡𝑖𝑒𝑠

In 2019, Apple’s inventories amounted to $4.106 billion. Thus, its 2019 quick ratio equaled (162.819 –  
4.106) / 105.718 = 1.50. This means that, not counting any cash that can be generated from selling its  
inventories, Apple had $1.50 available to cover every dollar of maturing bills. The industry average for  
the same year was 1.92 and Apple’s ratios were 1.33, 1.23, and 1.10 for 2016-2018, respectively. These  
ratios suggest similar conclusions as the current ratios.

**Asset Management Ratios**

These ratios measure how efficiently the company manages and uses its assets. Since the whole point of  
engaging in business is to generate revenues by selling some products or services, these ratios relate a  
company’s assets (used to generate revenues) to its revenue. Essentially, they seek to answer the  
question: Does the company have the right amount of assets relative to its sales? These ratios are  
typically calculated by dividing revenue by some measure of assets, including total assets, fixed assets,  
inventories, receivables, etc. We’ll consider four of them: inventory turnover, days sales outstanding,  
fixed assets turnover, and total assets turnover.

**Inventory Turnover Ratio**

This ratio measures how rapidly a company sells its inventories (i.e., goods held for sale). Our textbook  
uses the following formula:

𝐼𝑛𝑣𝑒𝑛𝑡𝑜𝑟𝑦 𝑇𝑢𝑟𝑛𝑜𝑣𝑒𝑟 = 𝑆𝑎𝑙𝑒𝑠 𝑅𝑒𝑣𝑒𝑛𝑢𝑒  
𝐼𝑛𝑣𝑒𝑛𝑡𝑜𝑟𝑖𝑒𝑠

You may encounter variations of this formula in other contexts, such as when the numerator is cost of  
goods sold rather than sales revenue. It’s probably more correct to use cost of goods sold in the  
numerator since inventories are carried at cost while revenue already includes mark-up. To be  
consistent with the textbook, I’ll use revenue in the numerator. What is most important is that ratios are  
calculated consistently over time and across companies when making comparisons.

In 2019, Apple’s revenue equaled $260.174 billion and its inventories totaled $4.106 billion. Thus, its  
inventory turnover ratio equaled 260.174/4.106 = 63.36. Imagine a warehouse filled with products sold  
by Apple and assume that the company starts selling from this warehouse without refilling it until  
everything is sold. After the warehouse is emptied, Apple fills it up again and begins selling. An inventory  
turnover of 63.36 means that the warehouse is emptied approximately 63 times during the year.  
Alternatively, we can say that each product spends an average of 5.8 days (365 days in a year divided by  
the inventory turnover ratio of 63.36) in the warehouse before being sold. The industry average  
inventory turnover ratio in 2019 was 10.26, and Apple’s ratios for 2016-2018 were 101.14, 47.22, and

67.14. Thus, Apple appears to carry a much lower average inventory relative to its revenue in  
comparison to the typical computer and electronics manufacturer.

A few comments are in order at this point. First, the inventory turnover ratio is more meaningful for  
some industries than others. Companies in the retail industry as well as those in manufacturing will pay  
a greater attention to the inventory turnover ratio than say service companies. Second, a high inventory  
turnover ratio does not necessarily mean that management is doing a great job of minimizing the capital  
tied down in inventories. It could well be the case that the company’s inventory management system is  
resulting in shortages, potentially driving customers away. Once again, the ratio serves as a pointer that  
allows us to ask the right questions.

**Days Sales Outstanding**

The days sales outstanding (DSO) measures the average number of days it takes a company to collect  
cash from products/services sold on credit. Typically, we’d like to see this number as low as possible  
because the longer it takes to collect cash from credit customers, the longer we have to tie down capital  
in receivables. Also, a longer DSO increases the potential for bad debts and incurring collection costs.  
Nevertheless, as with all ratios, we have to dig deeper to ensure that we’re reaching the right  
conclusions. For instance, a really low DSO could mean that the company is failing to make sales to some  
credit worthy customers, thereby foregoing potential profits. Or, it could mean that the company is  
offering too generous discount terms, which encourages customers to pay early.

The DSO is calculated as:

𝐷𝑎𝑦𝑠 𝑆𝑎𝑙𝑒𝑠 𝑂𝑢𝑡𝑠𝑡𝑎𝑛𝑑𝑖𝑛𝑔 = 𝐴𝑐𝑐𝑜𝑢𝑛𝑡𝑠 𝑅𝑒𝑐𝑒𝑖𝑣𝑎𝑏𝑙𝑒  
𝑆𝑎𝑙𝑒𝑠/365

In 2019, Apple had $22.926 billion in receivables. Since its revenue for 2019 was $260.174 billion, the  
DSO = 22.926 / (260.174/365) = 32.16 days. This means that Apple waited an average of 32 days to  
collect cash from its credit customers. Comparable numbers for previous years are 26.67 days in 2016,  
28.46 in 2017, and 31.86 in 2018. The industry average in 2019 was 60.29 days. Thus, while Apple  
collects cash from its credit customers much faster than the typical company in its industry, its DSO has  
increased over the past few years. Once again, this should cause us to ask questions regarding what is  
going on with the company’s credit customers.

**Fixed Assets Turnover Ratio**

Fixed assets are long-lived physical assets utilized by a company in operating its businesses. They include  
buildings, equipment, machines, vehicles, etc. The fixed assets turnover ratio measures the company’s  
efficiency in using these assets to generate sales. It is defined as the ratio of sales revenue to net fixed  
assets, usually shown as net property, plant, and equipment (PPE) on the balance sheet:

𝐹𝑖𝑥𝑒𝑑 𝐴𝑠𝑠𝑒𝑡𝑠 𝑇𝑢𝑟𝑛𝑜𝑣𝑒𝑟 = 𝑆𝑎𝑙𝑒𝑠  
𝑁𝑒𝑡 𝑃𝑃𝐸

For Apple, net fixed assets equaled $37.378 billion in 2019. Since 2019 sales revenue totaled $260.174  
billion, Apple’s fixed assets turnover ratio in 2019 was 260.174 / 37.378 = 6.96. This means that Apple  
generated $6.96 in revenue for every dollar of (net) property, plant, and equipment it owned in 2019.  
This compares to $7.98 in 2016, $6.79 in 2017, and $6.43 in 2018. The industry average for 2019 was  
14.34. Thus, Apple has suffered a slight decline in its operating efficiency over these four years and its  
fixed assets turnover is also below industry average.

In general, we’d like this ratio to be high, although again a high ratio is not necessarily a good thing. For  
example, a high ratio can be indicative of better efficiency or it could simply be a reflection of  
underinvestment in physical assets, resulting in significant over-usage of these assets and major  
repair/maintenance expenses in the future. It could also be the case that the company is fraudulently  
expensing capital assets rather than capitalizing and depreciating them over time. Thus, the ratio  
(whether high or low) should cause us to ask other questions and seek to understand why the ratio is  
what it is. Based on these other questions, we’d be able to reach an informed conclusion about the  
company’s efficiency.

**Total Assets Turnover Ratio**

The total assets turnover ratio is similar to the fixed assets turnover ratio. The only difference is that it  
measures the efficiency of all assets, rather than fixed assets as is the case with the fixed assets turnover  
ratio. It is defined as the ratio of sales revenue to the company’s total assets:

𝑇𝑜𝑡𝑎𝑙 𝐴𝑠𝑠𝑒𝑡𝑠 𝑇𝑢𝑟𝑛𝑜𝑣𝑒𝑟 = 𝑆𝑎𝑙𝑒𝑠  
𝑇𝑜𝑡𝑎𝑙 𝐴𝑠𝑠𝑒𝑡𝑠

In 2019, Apple’s total assets turnover ratio equaled 260.174 / 338.516 = 0.77. Thus, every dollar of  
assets owned by Apple in 2019 produced sales of $0.77. Comparable figures for previous years were  
$0.67 in 2016, $0.61 in 2017, and $0.73 in 2018. The trend is similar to that for fixed assets turnover,  
which is not surprising.

**Debt Management Ratios**

These ratios measure how much debt the company has and its ability to make required interest  
payments from its operating income. We’ll consider three of them:

1. Total debt ratio
2. Debt-to-capital ratio
3. Debt-equity ratio
4. Times interest earned (TIE) ratio

**Total Debt Ratio**

The total debt ratio measures how much of the company’s assets is financed with borrowed money. As  
we will discuss in later sessions, debt financing has its advantages but excessive debt can be quite  
damaging to shareholders’ interest. Thus, the trick here is to strike an appropriate balance.

There are several variations of the formula for calculating the total debt ratio. Here, we’ll stick with the  
one given in the textbook:

𝑇𝑜𝑡𝑎𝑙 𝐷𝑒𝑏𝑡 𝑅𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜 = 𝑆h𝑜𝑟𝑡-𝑡𝑒𝑟𝑚 + 𝐿𝑜𝑛𝑔-𝑡𝑒𝑟𝑚 𝐷𝑒𝑏𝑡  
𝑇𝑜𝑡𝑎𝑙 𝐴𝑠𝑠𝑒𝑡𝑠

Short-term debt includes notes payable, commercial papers, and current maturities of long-term debt.  
In 2019, Apple had no notes payable, $5.98 billion of commercial paper, and $10.26 billion of current  
maturities of long-term debt. Its long-term debt for the same year equaled $91.807 billion. Since Apple’s  
total assets during that year totaled $338.516 billion, its total debt ratio = (5.98 + 10.26 + 91.807) /  
338.516 = 0.32. Thus, approximately 32% of Apple’s assets were financed with some form of debt in  
2019. This compares with the industry average of 33%. Thus, Apple uses about the same level of debt as  
the typical company in its industry. Not only this, its debt ratio has been relatively stable but trending  
upwards, with debt ratios of 0.27 in 2016, and 0.31 in 2017, and 0.31 in 2018.

**Debt-to-Capital Ratio**

This ratio is very similar to the total debt ratio. It measures the percentage of a firm’s capital from  
investors that was provided by lenders/creditors. Recall that there are two major groups of investors in  
a company: lenders (or creditors) and shareholders. The debt-to-capital ratio measures the percentage  
of total capital supplied by lenders. The formula for calculating it is as follows:

𝐷𝑒𝑏𝑡 − 𝑡𝑜 − 𝐶𝑎𝑝𝑖𝑡𝑎𝑙 𝑅𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜 = 𝑆h𝑜𝑟𝑡-𝑡𝑒𝑟𝑚 + 𝐿𝑜𝑛𝑔-𝑡𝑒𝑟𝑚 𝐷𝑒𝑏𝑡  
𝑆h𝑜𝑟𝑡-𝑡𝑒𝑟𝑚 + 𝐿𝑜𝑛𝑔-𝑡𝑒𝑟𝑚 𝐷𝑒𝑏𝑡 + 𝑆h𝑎𝑟𝑒h𝑜𝑙𝑑𝑒𝑟𝑠′𝐸𝑞𝑢𝑖𝑡𝑦

For Apple in 2019, this ratio equaled (5.98 + 10.26 + 91.807) / (5.98 + 10.26 + 91.807 + 90.488) = 0.54.  
Thus, 54% of Apple’s total capital in 2019 was provided by creditors. This ratio has increased over time,  
from 0.40 (or 40%) in 2016 and is also above the industry average of 0.26. Thus, Apple appears to rely  
more on lenders than shareholders for its capital. One short-coming of this ratio is that it uses book  
values for both debt and equity. While the book value of debt is usually close to its market value, this is  
often not the case for equity.

**Debt-Equity Ratio**

The debt-equity ratio is essentially another way of expressing the debt-to-capital ratio. In this case, we  
compare the company’s debt with its equity. In other words, this ratio measures borrowed money in  
relation to the funds provided by the company’s owners, i.e., the shareholders. It is calculated as:

𝐷𝑒𝑏𝑡-𝐸𝑞𝑢𝑖𝑡𝑦 𝑅𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜 = 𝑆h𝑜𝑟𝑡-𝑡𝑒𝑟𝑚 + 𝐿𝑜𝑛𝑔-𝑡𝑒𝑟𝑚 𝐷𝑒𝑏𝑡  
𝑆h𝑎𝑟𝑒h𝑜𝑙𝑑𝑒𝑟𝑠′𝐸𝑞𝑢𝑖𝑡𝑦

For Apple, this equaled (5.98 + 10.26 + 91.807) / 90.488 = 1.19 in 2019. This means that Apple borrowed  
$1.19 for every dollar invested by shareholders in the company. As would be expected, this ratio  
exhibits the same trend as the total debt ratio.

**Times Interest Earned (TIE) Ratio**

This ratio measures a company’s ability to make interest payments from its operating income. It  
compares the amount the company has to pay in interest with the money available for making these  
payments. What the company has to pay in interest is simply its interest expense. Income available for  
making these payments is operating income or earnings before interest and taxes (EBIT). The TIE ratio is  
calculated as follows:

𝑇𝐼𝐸 𝑅𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜 = 𝑂𝑝𝑒𝑟𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑛𝑔 𝐼𝑛𝑐𝑜𝑚𝑒 (𝐸𝐵𝐼𝑇)  
𝐼𝑛𝑡𝑒𝑟𝑒𝑠𝑡 𝐸𝑥𝑝𝑒𝑛𝑠𝑒

Using Apple’s 2019 numbers, interest expenses equaled $3.576 billion, while operating income equaled  
$63.93 billion. Thus, the TIE ratio equaled 63.93 / 3.576= 17.88 times. Thus, Apple had $17.88 available  
in operating income to pay every dollar of interest in 2019. This is much higher than the industry  
average of 7.72 in 2019 and suggests that Apple has large room to take on additional debt if needed.  
Note that this ratio has trended downwards as Apple increased its debt over the years.

**Profitability Ratios**

These ratios assist in the assessment of a company’s profitability. There are several of them, but we’ll be  
focusing on the four more important ones:

1. Basic earnings power
2. Profit margin
3. Return on assets
4. Return on equity

**Basic Earnings Powers (BEP)**

This ratio measures the profitability of a company’s basic operations, before considering the effects of  
other sources of income and expenses that are unrelated to the company’s core activities. This is a very  
important ratio because it allows us to understand the ability of the company to generate income from  
its normal operations. As you will see later when we consider the decision to finance operations with  
debt, the BEP plays a significant role in determining whether it is worthwhile for a company to use debt  
in funding its activities. In general, if BEP is greater than the interest rate to be paid on debt, then using  
debt will be beneficial to shareholders.

The BEP is calculated as the ratio of operating income (EBIT) to total assets. The formula is as follows:

𝐵𝐸𝑃 = 𝑂𝑝𝑒𝑟𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑛𝑔 𝐼𝑛𝑐𝑜𝑚𝑒 (𝐸𝐵𝐼𝑇)  
𝑇𝑜𝑡𝑎𝑙 𝐴𝑠𝑠𝑒𝑡𝑠

The BEP for Apple in 2019 equaled 63.93 / 338.516 = 18.9%. Thus, every dollar invested by Apple in its  
assets generated 18.9 cents in operating income in 2019. This means that Apple can potentially increase  
its net income if it can borrow money at an interest rate lower than 18.9%. This ratio is relatively stable  
over the 2016-2019 period and is significantly above the industry average of 2.1% in 2019.

**Profit Margin**

The profit margin measures how much of each dollar of revenue ends up in the residual that belongs to  
shareholders. Recall the money pot example given earlier. The pot represents total revenue. The profit  
margin measures how much is left in the pot after everyone with a claim has been paid. In general, the  
higher the profit margin, the better off the shareholders.

The profit margin is calculated as the ratio of net income to sales revenue as follows:

𝑃𝑟𝑜𝑓𝑖𝑡 𝑀𝑎𝑟𝑔𝑖𝑛 = 𝑁𝑒𝑡 𝐼𝑛𝑐𝑜𝑚𝑒  
𝑆𝑎𝑙𝑒𝑠 𝑅𝑒𝑣𝑒𝑛𝑢𝑒

In 2019, Apple’s profit margin equaled 55.256 / 260.174 = 21.2%. This implies that 21.2 cents of every  
dollar of revenue generated by Apple in 2019 is left for shareholders after everyone else has been paid.  
Similar to the basic earnings power, this ratio has remained relatively stable over the years and is well  
above the industry average.

**Return on Assets (ROA)**

The ROA attempts to measure the rate of return generated by the company on its assets. It is calculated  
as the ratio of net income to total assets:

𝑅𝑂𝐴= 𝑁𝑒𝑡𝐼𝑛𝑐𝑜𝑚𝑒  
𝑇𝑜𝑡𝑎𝑙 𝐴𝑠𝑠𝑒𝑡𝑠

Since Apple’s 2019 net income and total assets were $55.256 billion and $338.516 billion, respectively,  
its ROA equaled 55.256 / 338.516 = 16.3% in 2019, up from a low of 12.9% in 2017 and compared to  
0.5% for the industry. Once again, this is consistent with the other profitability ratios in suggesting that  
Apple’s profitability is better than the industry’s.

**Return on Equity (ROE)**

The ROE is quite similar to the ROA in that it attempts to measure a rate of return. In this case, however,  
we’re trying to measure a rate of return to the shareholders. It is calculated as the ratio of net income to  
shareholders’ equity. Shareholders’ equity is the cumulative amount invested by shareholders in the  
company since its inception. By dividing the company’s net income by this amount, the ROE seeks to  
establish the rate of return earned by shareholders during the year. The ROE is calculated as follows:

𝑅𝑂𝐸 = 𝑁𝑒𝑡 𝐼𝑛𝑐𝑜𝑚𝑒  
𝑆h𝑎𝑟𝑒h𝑜𝑙𝑑𝑒𝑟𝑠′𝐸𝑞𝑢𝑖𝑡𝑦

In 2019, Apple’s shareholders’ equity equaled $90.488 billion. Given a net income of $55.256 billion, the  
company’s ROE in 2019 equaled 55.256 / 90.488 = 61.1%. This ratio has increased significantly over  
time, rising from a low of 35.6% in 2016.

The ROE gives us an overall rate of return earned by the company’s shareholders during a particular  
year. This overall rate of return depends on three major factors. Thus, we can decompose the ROE into  
three corresponding constituents to gain a better insight into the factors affecting the company’s

financial performance. According to the DuPont equation, we can express a company’s ROE as a product  
of its total assets turnover, profit margin, and leverage multiplier as follows:

𝑅𝑂𝐸 = 𝑆𝑎𝑙𝑒𝑠 × 𝑁𝑒𝑡 𝐼𝑛𝑐𝑜𝑚𝑒 × 𝑇𝑜𝑡𝑎𝑙 𝐴𝑠𝑠𝑒𝑡𝑠  
𝑇𝑜𝑡𝑎𝑙 𝐴𝑠𝑠𝑒𝑡𝑠 𝑆𝑎𝑙𝑒𝑠 𝑆h𝑎𝑟𝑒h𝑜𝑙𝑑𝑒𝑟𝑠′𝐸𝑞𝑢𝑖𝑡𝑦

Study this equation for a minute. Basically, it says the return earned by shareholders (i.e., the ROE)  
depends first on how efficiently the company uses its assets to generate revenue (i.e., the total assets  
turnover), then on how well the company manages its expenses (i.e., how much of each dollar of  
revenue ends up in the residual belonging to shareholders, the profit margin), and finally on the extent  
to which the company uses debt to magnify the return to shareholders (i.e., the leverage multiplier).  
This equation also suggests that there are several ways to improve a company’s ROE. We could focus on  
improving efficiency, reducing costs, or changing leverage. We also can use the DuPont equation to dig  
deeper in seeking to understand the drivers of a company’s financial performance.

Let’s get back to Apple. As stated before, its sales, total assets, net income, and shareholders’ equity in  
2019 were $260.174 billion, $338.516 billion, $55.256 billion, and $90.488 billion, respectively. Thus, we  
can decompose Apple’s 2019 ROE as follows:

𝑅𝑂𝐸 = 𝑆𝑎𝑙𝑒𝑠 × 𝑁𝑒𝑡 𝐼𝑛𝑐𝑜𝑚𝑒 × 𝑇𝑜𝑡𝑎𝑙 𝐴𝑠𝑠𝑒𝑡𝑠 = 260.174 × 55.256 × 338.516  
𝑇𝑜𝑡𝑎𝑙 𝐴𝑠𝑠𝑒𝑡𝑠 𝑆𝑎𝑙𝑒𝑠 𝑆h𝑎𝑟𝑒h𝑜𝑙𝑑𝑒𝑟𝑠′𝐸𝑞𝑢𝑖𝑡𝑦 338.516 260.174 90.488

Thus, we obtain the same ROE of 61.1% as before as follows (numbers not exact due to rounding):

𝑅𝑂𝐸 = 0.769×0.212×3.741 = 0.61 = 61%

The beauty of this is that it allows us to understand drivers of changes in a company’s ROE over time. In  
the case of Apple, its ROE increased from 35.6% in 2016 to 36.1%, 55.6%, and 61.1% in 2017, 2018, and  
2019, respectively. By evaluating the different components of ROE, we can see that the major factor  
behind this trend is Apple’s increasing leverage during the period. Its profit margin remained stable at  
around 21% while its total assets turnover increased marginally from 0.67 in 2016 to 0.77 in 2019. In  
contrast, its debt-equity ratio increased significantly, from 0.68 in 2016 to 1.19 in 2019.

**Market Value Ratios**

The ratios we have considered so far have all focused exclusively on figures contained in the financial  
statements. While these numbers are obviously important, they suffer from the major disadvantage that  
they’re all accounting numbers, prepared under the typical historical cost accounting convention. For  
example, the fact that two companies have the same assets turnover could be meaningless if the  
companies purchased their assets at different points in time. Market value ratios seek to correct these  
problems by focusing on market numbers rather than accounting numbers. We’ll consider two of these  
ratios:

a. Price-earnings (PE) ratio

b. Market/book ratio

**Price-Earnings Ratio**

This is the ratio of a company’s stock price to its earnings per share. Essentially, it measures how much  
investors are willing to pay for each dollar of net income earned by the company. If you think about it,  
the only reason the PE ratio should differ across companies or over time is simply because of investors’  
expectation about the company’s future prospects.

The PE ratio is calculated as follows:

𝑃𝐸 𝑅𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜 = 𝑃𝑟𝑖𝑐𝑒 𝑃𝑒𝑟 𝑆h𝑎𝑟𝑒  
𝐸𝑎𝑟𝑛𝑖𝑛𝑔𝑠 𝑃𝑒𝑟 𝑆h𝑎𝑟𝑒

As of the end of its 2019 fiscal year (September 28, 2019), Apple’s stock price was $248.76, while its  
basic earnings per share for the year was $11.97. Thus, its PE ratio equaled 248.76/11.97 = 20.79. Thus,  
investors were willing to pay $20.79 for every dollar of earnings generated by Apple in 2019.

**Market/book Ratio**

This is the ratio of the market value of the company’s equity to its book value. The book value of equity  
(that is, shareholders’ equity) is the total of all funds invested by shareholders in the company since its  
inception until the relevant balance sheet date, measured at cost. Thus, Apple’s shareholders’ equity of  
$90.488 billion as of September 28, 2019 means that since inception until 09/28/19, Apple’s  
shareholders have invested a total of $90.488 billion in the company, measured at cost and net of any  
amounts returned to shareholders in the form of stock repurchases. The market value of equity is simply  
the company’s market capitalization, calculated as the product of the number of shares outstanding and  
the price per share. By comparing the market and book value of shareholders’ equity, the market/book  
ratio measures the current worth of each dollar of shareholders’ investment in the company. What do  
you think a market/book ratio of less than 1.0 imply? Do you think there are companies with  
market/book ratio less than 1.0? If so, do you think this is sustainable?

The market/book ratio is calculated as follows:

𝑀𝑎𝑟𝑘𝑒𝑡/𝑏𝑜𝑜𝑘 𝑅𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜 = 𝑃𝑟𝑖𝑐𝑒 𝑃𝑒𝑟 𝑆h𝑎𝑟𝑒×𝑆h𝑎𝑟𝑒𝑠 𝑂𝑢𝑡𝑠𝑡𝑎𝑛𝑑𝑖𝑛𝑔  
𝑆h𝑎𝑟𝑒h𝑜𝑙𝑑𝑒𝑟𝑠′𝐸𝑞𝑢𝑖𝑡𝑦

Apple had 4.618 billion shares outstanding on September 28, 2019. Given that its market price was  
$248.76 per share and shareholders’ equity totaled $90.488 billion, this means that its market/book  
ratio in 2019 was (248.76 × 4.618) / 90.488 = 12.69. Thus, each dollar invested by shareholders in Apple  
was worth $12.69 at the end of 2019.

## Conclusion

Ratios can be quite useful in understanding a company's financial health and performance. As stated previously, their usefulness derives mostly from their ability to point us to the right questions to ask to uncover a company's strengths and weaknesses. Despite this usefulness, ratios do not tell us everything and the following factors limit their value:

1. It is always good to compare a company's ratios with its industry's average ratio. Yet, comparison with industry averages is difficult if the firm operates many different divisions. For example, with which industry would you compare GE's ratios? Besides, the industry average is not necessarily a satisfactory target to shoot for.
2. Differences in operating and accounting policies, seasonal factors, and inflation can further distort comparisons.
3. Window dressing techniques can make financial statements and ratios look better. An analyst must understand how to identify and correct such attempts to make ratios more useful.
